Complete Protest Record

From Sit-In to
National Movement

What began in 2022 as localized protests in Rawalakot over wheat prices has grown into the most significant civil society uprising in Azad Kashmir's modern history — with two major national-level confrontations, 13 civilian lives lost, and a landmark government agreement extracted through sustained peaceful resistance.

2022
–2023
Origins
Local Trader & Transport Protests — The Awakening Begins
Rising commodity and electricity prices trigger localized, uncoordinated protests across AJK led by traders' associations and transport unions. Activists symbolically burn electricity bills and hold shutter-down strikes and sit-ins — spreading from Rawalakot to other districts. Pakistan was experiencing severe economic turmoil in 2022 with inflation at 38% and devastating floods, amplifying AJK's existing grievances about energy costs and food prices.
The AJK government attempts limited reconciliation — withdrawing a proposed electricity bill increase and maintaining tariffs at the July 2023 level, reportedly burdening the local exchequer by ₨10–12 billion. However, critics note the far more favorable subsidies provided to Gilgit-Baltistan, and public discontent remains high. The movement begins to self-organize around digital networks.
Foundation laid for what would become a region-wide, multi-sector coalition with the power to challenge the Federal Government of Pakistan directly.
2023
Sep–Oct
Formation
JKJAAC Formally Consolidates as Cross-Regional Coalition
Multiple local committees and traders' bodies formalize their coordination into the broader Jammu Kashmir Joint Awami Action Committee by mid-September 2023. The committee's first major test comes on October 10, 2023, when it organizes large, inclusive rallies across all major towns of AJK — notably including women and children — demonstrating the movement's broad social reach beyond just traders and transporters. Protests also spread to the AJK diaspora in the UK.
The resilience of the new coalition is tested during difficult winter months. In November 2023, JKJAAC stages a major protest that ends after the AJK government provides assurances. However, the government's subsequent failure to implement these promises becomes the direct and central trigger for the far more explosive mass actions that follow in 2024.
Formal establishment of JKJAAC as a region-wide, cross-sectoral civil society platform. A call for strike is also given for February 5, 2024 (Kashmir Solidarity Day).
2023
May
Catalyst
Rawalakot Flour Depot Sit-In — The Organizational Nucleus
A sustained sit-in outside a flour depot in Supply Bazaar, Rawalakot — targeting the soaring price of wheat — becomes the defining early moment of what will become JKJAAC. This localized but disciplined protest demonstrates the potential for civil society to organize around immediate economic hardship. Leadership begins to emerge from across the traders' community, with social media enabling real-time coordination across districts previously unable to act in concert.
Proved the organizing model of sustained, peaceful civil disobedience that would eventually unite all districts of AJK under a single platform.

The 2024 Azad Kashmir Protests

In May 2024, a six-day wave of protests swept AJK in what became the most significant mass demonstrations the region had seen in years. What began as demands for subsidised flour and lower electricity tariffs escalated into a full-scale confrontation — ending with a landmark ₨23 billion federal concession and permanently changing AJK's political landscape.

2024
May 8
Uprising
Six-Day Mass Uprising Begins — Long March Called
A six-day wave of protests, sit-ins, shutter-downs, and wheel-jam strikes sweeps AJK beginning May 8. JAAC emerges as the principal organiser and interlocutor. Recognizing the momentum, the Muzaffarabad Public Action Committees call for a "long march" toward the capital on May 11, 2024. The federal government mobilizes 600 Punjab Constabulary personnel and Frontier Constabulary in a bid to block the march. The AJK government declares a two-day holiday for "security reasons."
Largest mobilization in AJK in recent memory — including participants from all 10 districts, all professions, and all demographic groups including women and youth.
2024
May 8–9
Crackdown
Government Arrests 70 Activists — Massively Backfires
In the intervening night of May 8–9, police arrest around 70 JKJAAC activists after raiding residences in Muzaffarabad and Mirpur divisions. Arrested include Shaukat Nawaz Mir and multiple core committee members. In Mirpur's Dadyal Tehsil, a dozen activists are arrested, provoking immediate clashes with traders. The government deploys 5,500 police. The Inspector General of Police makes false allegations of Indian Intelligence involvement.
The crackdown massively backfires — inflaming public anger to unprecedented levels and triggering the most intense phase of protests. Prohibitory orders under section 144 are issued across Mirpur district, banning gatherings of five or more persons.
Government's attempt at suppression produces the opposite effect — dramatically expanding the scale and intensity of protests, with nationwide sympathy mobilized.
2024
May 13
Victory
PM Shehbaz Announces ₨23 Billion — Historic Victory
Confrontations between protesters and law enforcement result in at least three protester deaths and one police officer killed. Amid mounting national and international pressure, on May 13, 2024, Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announces a ₨23 billion (~$82 million) federal grant for Azad Kashmir. AJK Premier Chaudhry Anwar-ul-Haq announces immediate subsidies and reduced utility rates.
On May 14, JKJAAC announces suspension of major actions following the concessions, declaring it a "historic victory." Wheat flour prices are cut from ₨3,100 to ₨2,000 per 40kg. Electricity rates are restructured: ₨3/unit (1–100), ₨5/unit (100–300), ₨6/unit (300+).
Wheat: ₨3,100 → ₨2,000/40kg | Electricity: ₨3/unit min | Judicial Commission formed to review official privileges | ~$82 million federal grant for AJK development.

The 2025 Azad Kashmir Protests —
A Nation in Lockdown

After the government failed to fully implement the 2024 promises and then introduced a repressive ordinance banning unregistered public gatherings, JKJAAC returned with a comprehensive 38-Point Charter. From September 29 to October 4, 2025, AJK was brought to a complete standstill in the most intense confrontation yet — resulting in 10 martyrs, an internet blackout, and ultimately a landmark agreement.

10

Martyrs (min. confirmed)

At least 10 protesters were confirmed dead in the violent clashes that erupted during the September 2025 protests, with some injured later succumbing to their wounds.

100+

People Injured

Dozens, likely numbering over 100, were injured across the region due to gunfire, clashes between rival groups, and shelling .

6

Days of Total Lockdown

The JAAC enforced a complete, region-wide lockdown ("shutter-down" strike) from September 29 to October 4, 2025, paralyzing life in Azad Jammu and Kashmir .

Oct 4

Agreement Signed

On October 4, 2025, the Pakistani government and the JAAC signed a formal agreement in Islamabad, ending the six days of unrest and violence .

2025
Jun–Aug
New Charter
38-Point Charter Developed Through Public Consultation
Following the government's failure to implement the December 2024 Kohala agreement (only 1 of 12 agreed demands acted upon), JKJAAC announces its expanded 38-Point Charter of Demands developed through public consultation across all districts. The new charter expands the movement's scope beyond economic demands to include constitutional reforms, abolition of refugee legislative seats, free healthcare and education, and diaspora rights.
The AJK government, at Pakistan's direction, introduces the "Peaceful Assembly and Public Order Ordinance 2024" requiring prior government permission for public gatherings. The ordinance sparks a fierce backlash — even AJK's own President eventually calls his signed ordinance a "black law" under public and judicial pressure. Mass resistance forces its withdrawal.
38-Point Charter finalized. Movement's ideological scope expanded. AJK's repressive ordinance forced into withdrawal through sustained resistance — a democratic victory in itself.
2025
Sep 28–29
Blackout
Internet Blackout — But March Proceeds Anyway
On September 27, PM Shahbaz Sharif publicly urges JKJAAC to withdraw its call for an indefinite lockdown and return to talks. JKJAAC refuses. On September 28, Pakistan's Ministry of Interior orders mobile and internet services suspended across major parts of AJK — a direct attempt to prevent coordination. On September 29, shutdown and wheel-jam strikes begin in Muzaffarabad, Rawalakot, Kotli, Mirpur, Bhimber, and other cities despite the blackout.
Entry and exit points across AJK are sealed. A rival "peace rally" by the Muslim Conference (pro-government) clashes with JKJAAC protesters at Neelum Bridge in Muzaffarabad. Gunfire by Raja Saqib Majeed and his brother leaves several injured; one protester, Sudheer, is killed — the first martyr of the 2025 uprising.
The communications blackout — unprecedented in AJK's history — confirms the state's recognition of JKJAAC's advanced organizational capacity and the scale of public support behind the movement.
2025
Oct 1–2
Martyrs
Bloody Clashes — Multiple Deaths in Muzaffarabad
On October 1–2, deadly clashes break out between JKJAAC protesters and law enforcement in Muzaffarabad. According to a police statement, three policemen and one civilian were killed in the Muzaffarabad confrontations. Protest leaders report that at least 12 JKJAAC supporters were killed across AJK and more than 200 injured. Some senior officials put the death toll at 15. The government reports 172 police personnel injured, 12 critically.
Despite the violence, protesters hold their ground. The AJK government, recognizing the crisis cannot be resolved through force, issues an appeal to JKJAAC to return to talks. International observers and the AJK diaspora in the UK issue emergency statements calling for protection of protesters.
Total confirmed civilian deaths in 2024–2025 protest cycle: at least 13. Total protest period: March 2022 to October 2025. The unprecedented sacrifice of human life forces the federal government to the negotiating table.
2025
Oct 4
Agreement
13-Hour Marathon Negotiations — Historic Agreement Signed
After five days of violence, 10 confirmed martyrs, an internet blackout, and 13 hours of marathon negotiations, a high-level federal delegation reaches a preliminary agreement with JKJAAC in Muzaffarabad on October 4, 2025. PM Shehbaz Sharif hails the agreement as resolving "buried conspiracies." JKJAAC declares it a people's victory achieved through sacrifice and steadfast resistance.
Key agreement points: compensation for martyrs' families, ₨1 million per injured person, withdrawal of 177 of 192 FIRs, reinstatement of suspended employees, cabinet reduction to 20 members, formation of legal committee to address refugee seats, restoration of health cards and mobile services.
By February 2026, Federal Minister Amir Muqam confirms 31 of 37 points implemented. JKJAAC disputes the characterization, stating health cards remain unrestored and internet restrictions continue.

DISRUPTION PROOF: THE MOVEMENT THAT WOULDN’T BE SILENCED

The Anatomy of the
JKJAAC Uprising

Undeterred by the 2025 internet shutdown, JKJAAC activated deep-rooted offline networks and international diaspora support. By framing demands within constitutional law and backing a historic AJK Police strike, the movement turned digital suppression into a demonstration of unshakable organizational power.

The Power of Social Media

JKJAAC's organizing proceeded as planned — demonstrating that the movement had built organizational infrastructure resilient to state interference. Pre-positioned coordinators, offline communication networks, and the deep community trust built over three years made digital suppression ineffective.

Diaspora Solidarity

The AJK diaspora — particularly the large community in Birmingham, Bradford, and London — played a crucial role in maintaining international pressure during both the 2024 and 2025 uprisings. UK-based activists organized solidarity protests, lobbied British MPs, and provided crucial media coverage when Pakistani media was suppressing the story.

AJK Police Strike — July 2025

In a stunning development, AJK Police personnel themselves went on strike in July 2025 demanding better pay and conditions — adopting JKJAAC slogans. JKJAAC's Core Committee issued a statement of full support for the police strike. This underscored how the movement had transcended its origins to become a symbol of resistance for all government workers exploited by the system.

Constitutional & Legal Strategy

JKJAAC's legal team consistently framed demands within Pakistan's own constitutional framework and international law. By invoking UN resolutions on Kashmir's disputed status, the UDHR, and the ICCPR, the movement built a case that government suppression was not just politically unwise but legally impermissible under both Pakistani and international law.
