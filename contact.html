At KFM, we believe in transparency and community engagement. Below are answers to frequently asked questions about our mission and how you can get involved.

9 Questions Answered
The JKJAAC, often abbreviated as JAAC, is a grassroots civil-society coalition and socio-political organization formed in Pakistan-administered Azad Jammu and Kashmir (AJK). It brings together various civic groups—including traders, transporters, lawyers, and student organizations—to advocate for economic and governance reforms. It emerged from local protests in 2022-2023 and was formalized as a cross-regional body in September 2023.
The core objective is to address long-standing economic hardship and governance failures in AJK. Its demands are encapsulated in a comprehensive 38-point Charter of Demands, which broadly falls into two categories:
  • Immediate Economic Relief: Reduction in electricity tariffs, subsidized wheat flour prices.
  • Long-term Structural Reforms: Abolition of elite privileges for officials, and the removal of the 12 reserved "refugee seats" in the AJK Legislative Assembly.
The JKJAAC was officially founded on September 16, 2023. Its key leaders include:
  • Shoukat Nawaz Mir: The de facto leader and Chairman.
  • Hafeez Hamdani: The official Spokesperson.
  • Raja Amjad Ali & Anjum Zaman Awan: Core members and negotiators.
The JKJAAC has organized two major waves of widespread unrest:
  • May 2024 Protests: A six-day wave of protests over rising inflation and electricity prices that led to a government subsidy package.
  • September-October 2025 Protests: A more intense movement involving an indefinite "lockdown" strike, which resulted in a communications blackout, deadly clashes, and ultimately a landmark agreement with the government.
The October 2025 protests ended with a landmark agreement signed on October 4, 2025. Key outcomes included compensation for families of those killed, a reduction of cabinet ministers from 36 to 20, reinstatement of the Local Government Act of 1990, and the formation of a constitutional committee to decide on the issues of refugee seats and elite privileges.
As of early 2026, the government reports that 31 out of 37 points in the agreement have been completed or are progressing. However, the JKJAAC has expressed concerns about the slow progress on core political issues, such as the abolition of refugee seats, and has called for continued dialogue to ensure all structural demands are met.
The 12 reserved seats for Kashmiri refugees are controversial because the JKJAAC views them as an undemocratic tool that allows federal influence over AJK's politics. Critics argue these representatives monopolize development funds and political patronage at the expense of the local population. The government's reluctance to abolish them stems from its desire to maintain a constitutional link with refugees originally from Indian-administered Kashmir.
The government's response has been two-pronged:
  • Dialogue and Concessions: High-level delegations have held talks resulting in significant economic packages and signed agreements.
  • Security and Control: During intense protests, authorities have resorted to arresting leaders and imposing communications blackouts to maintain public order.
For further details, you can refer to news reports from reputable Pakistani and international news agencies (such as the Associated Press of Pakistan), independent analyses from publications like The News International, or the comprehensive overview available on the JKJAAC Wikipedia page.
